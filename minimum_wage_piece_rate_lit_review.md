
# Literature Review

## 1. Minimum Wages and Worker Productivity

A large empirical literature studies the labor‑market effects of minimum wage policies, focusing primarily on employment, hours, and wage distribution. Classic contributions exploit variation in minimum wage policies across time and regions to estimate their causal effects on labor market outcomes. While this literature has produced extensive evidence on employment responses, comparatively less attention has been devoted to **worker productivity and effort responses**.

In principle, minimum wages may affect productivity through several channels. Higher wage floors may induce firms to substitute toward more productive workers or capital, or they may alter worker incentives and effort. In efficiency‑wage frameworks, for example, higher wages can increase productivity by improving worker morale or reducing turnover. Empirical evidence consistent with these channels has been documented in several contexts. For example, Coviello et al. study retail workers and find that increases in minimum wages can raise individual worker productivity, interpreting the result through improved morale and lower turnover.

Despite these contributions, identifying productivity responses to minimum wages remains challenging because individual output is rarely observed directly in most industries. As a result, relatively few studies are able to directly measure how minimum wage policies affect worker productivity or effort.

## 2. Minimum Wages in Piece‑Rate Settings

Piece‑rate compensation systems provide a particularly useful environment for studying productivity responses to minimum wages because individual output can be observed directly. In many such systems, workers are paid based on output but are guaranteed a minimum hourly wage if piece‑rate earnings fall below the statutory floor. This creates a nonlinear compensation structure in which the marginal return to effort depends on whether piece‑rate earnings exceed the minimum wage threshold.

Two influential empirical studies examine the interaction between minimum wages and piece‑rate compensation.

Hill studies strawberry harvesters in California who are paid piece rates but must receive at least the statutory minimum wage. She develops a model in which the minimum wage creates a region where the marginal return to effort is zero whenever piece‑rate earnings fall below the wage floor. Empirically, she finds that increases in the minimum wage reduce worker productivity, consistent with the prediction that minimum wage floors weaken marginal incentives in piece‑rate systems.

Ku studies tomato pickers in Florida using detailed personnel data on individual output. Exploiting a state minimum wage increase, she finds that low‑productivity workers increase effort following the policy change. In this setting, workers whose productivity is near the minimum wage threshold appear to increase effort in order to avoid triggering costly employer top‑ups.

Together, these studies demonstrate that minimum wages can affect productivity in piece‑rate environments, although they emphasize different mechanisms driving worker responses.

## 3. Incentives and Performance Pay

A closely related literature in personnel economics examines how incentive pay affects worker productivity. A central result in this literature is that workers respond strongly to marginal incentives embedded in compensation contracts.

Lazear studies a firm that switched from hourly wages to piece‑rate compensation and finds large increases in worker productivity following the introduction of performance pay. Importantly, he shows that productivity gains arise from both increased effort and worker selection.

Shearer studies tree‑planting workers in British Columbia and uses experimental variation in compensation schemes to estimate structural responses to incentive pay. His results show that workers exert significantly greater effort under piece‑rate contracts than under fixed wages, highlighting the importance of marginal incentives in shaping worker behavior.

These studies establish that incentive pay strongly influences productivity, but they largely examine settings without minimum wage floors and therefore do not analyze how regulatory wage guarantees interact with piece‑rate incentives.

## 4. Contribution of This Paper

This paper contributes to the literature by examining how minimum wage policies interact with piece‑rate incentives in an environment with **heterogeneous tasks and stochastic productivity**.

Using detailed data on tree‑planting workers paid under piece‑rate contracts, the paper develops and implements a measure of the **probability that the minimum wage floor binds** for a given worker and contract. This probability captures the likelihood that piece‑rate earnings fall below the statutory minimum wage and therefore that the marginal return to effort collapses.

The paper makes three primary contributions.

First, the analysis introduces a **binding‑probability framework** for studying minimum wage distortions in piece‑rate environments. Rather than relying solely on observed productivity levels or worker characteristics, the paper directly measures the likelihood that the minimum wage floor constrains earnings.

Second, the study incorporates **task heterogeneity and environmental shocks**, such as contract difficulty and weather conditions, which affect productivity risk and therefore the probability that the minimum wage becomes binding.

Third, by combining detailed worker‑level productivity data with policy variation across jurisdictions, the paper provides new evidence on how minimum wage policies alter incentives within performance‑based compensation systems.

Taken together, the results show that the effects of minimum wage increases on worker productivity depend critically on the **probability that the wage floor binds**, providing new insight into how minimum wage regulations interact with incentive pay.
